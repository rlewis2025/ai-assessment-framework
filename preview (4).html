Overview / Framework
Institutional language

One capability framework with three assessment standards.

Institutions often use these terms interchangeably. A disciplined framework separates foundational understanding, applied practice, and measurable value creation.

Level 101

AI Literacy

Foundational understanding of what AI is, how it works at a conceptual level, where it is useful, where it can fail, and how to use it responsibly.

Assessment question: Do they understand AI?
Level 202

AI Fluency

Practical ability to use AI tools effectively by prompting, refining, comparing outputs, verifying claims, and integrating AI into real workflows.

Assessment question: Can they work with AI?
Level 303

AI Competency

Consistent ability to produce high-quality outcomes with AI in academic, professional, research, teaching, or operational contexts.

Assessment question: Can they create value with AI?
LiteracyKnowledge and judgment
FluencyApplication and workflow
CompetencyPerformance and value

Overview / Audiences
Audience strategy

Apply one model while preserving role-specific evidence.

Students, faculty, and professional staff all need AI capability, but the evidence of learning should look different for each group.

ST

Students

Focus on academic integrity, career readiness, responsible tool use, and discipline-specific problem solving.

  • Orientation modules and knowledge checks
  • Course-based AI assignments and simulations
  • Portfolios, capstones, internships, and applied projects
FA

Faculty

Focus on teaching design, assessment integrity, scholarship, advising, and ethical integration into learning environments.

  • Responsible-use certification and case discussions
  • AI-enhanced assignment redesign
  • Evidence of improved learning, feedback, or research workflows
PS

Professional Staff

Focus on productivity, service quality, communication, decision support, student support, and process improvement.

  • Data privacy and institutional policy scenarios
  • Workflow demonstrations and tool selection exercises
  • Documented before-and-after operational improvements

Overview / Assessment
Assessment architecture

Match evidence to capability level.

A quiz can measure literacy, but it cannot prove fluency or competency. A strong campus approach combines knowledge checks, authentic tasks, and evidence of meaningful outcomes.

AI Literacy

Knowledge check

Use short modules, quizzes, and scenario-based questions to confirm baseline understanding.

  • AI definitions and terminology
  • Hallucinations and verification
  • Privacy, bias, copyright, and security
  • Institutional policies and appropriate use
AI Fluency

Performance task

Use authentic exercises where participants must prompt, iterate, evaluate, and revise.

  • Prompt design and refinement
  • Output comparison and critique
  • Evidence checking and source review
  • Integration into a course, research, or work process
AI Competency

Evidence portfolio

Use deliverables and impact evidence to show that AI has improved outcomes.

  • Final product or process artifact
  • Documented human judgment
  • Responsible-use reflection
  • Quality, efficiency, learning, or service impact

Overview / Rubric
Campus rubric

Assess the behaviors that matter.

This rubric can be adapted for a course assignment, professional development pathway, workshop, badge, or campus certificate.

Understanding
BeginningRecognizes AI tools but has limited understanding of strengths, weaknesses, or risks.
DevelopingExplains basic AI concepts and identifies obvious use cases and limitations.
ProficientConnects AI concepts to responsible use, verification, and human oversight.
AdvancedApplies nuanced judgment to risks, policy, ethics, and domain context.
Use of AI
BeginningUses a single prompt and accepts output with little review.
DevelopingUses clearer prompts and performs basic edits.
ProficientIterates, compares outputs, checks claims, and adapts tool use to the task.
AdvancedDesigns reliable workflows that combine AI output with expert human judgment.
Quality of outcome
BeginningOutput is generic, incomplete, or weakly aligned to purpose.
DevelopingOutput is useful but requires substantial human correction.
ProficientOutput is accurate, polished, and appropriate for the intended audience.
AdvancedOutput creates measurable improvement in learning, work quality, insight, or efficiency.
Responsible practice
BeginningShows limited attention to privacy, bias, attribution, or policy.
DevelopingIdentifies common risks but may not address them consistently.
ProficientUses AI transparently, protects sensitive data, and verifies important claims.
AdvancedModels responsible adoption and helps others make informed AI decisions.

Overview / Roadmap
Adoption roadmap

A phased path for campus adoption.

Start with a common language, pilot in a few high-value contexts, and scale through reusable rubrics and evidence portfolios.

Define

  • Adopt shared definitions
  • Clarify policy boundaries
  • Select priority audiences

Pilot

  • Run a literacy module
  • Test performance tasks
  • Gather feedback

Credential

  • Create badges or certificates
  • Publish rubrics
  • Document evidence standards

Improve

  • Review outcomes
  • Update for new tools
  • Scale communities of practice

Overview / Share
Recognition model

Use a four-level recognition model.

Credentialing makes the framework visible, portable, and easy to communicate across departments and programs.

1

AI Literate

Understands AI concepts, risks, and responsible use expectations.

2

AI Fluent

Uses AI effectively in authentic academic or workplace tasks.

3

AI Competent

Produces high-quality, value-adding outcomes with AI.

4

AI Leader

Mentors others, designs workflows, evaluates tools, and promotes responsible innovation.

Guiding principle

The strongest AI assessment frameworks move beyond asking whether someone knows about AI or can use a tool. They evaluate whether people can apply AI responsibly to solve meaningful problems, exercise sound judgment, and create measurable value.
